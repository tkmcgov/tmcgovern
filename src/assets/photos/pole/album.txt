description: The 2025 austral winter at the Amundsen-Scott South Pole Station marked the 69th winter season of continuous operations, and yet less 2000 people have been at pole in the winter. I spent just shy of a year there, arriving in December 2024 and departing in November 2025. Pole typically has ~150 people at any given time in the summer, with multiple flights most weeks. However come February, temperatures start to drop to the point where LC-130s can no longer support us. When the last flight leaves, a skeleton crew of ~40 (42 my winter) stay behind to keep the station afloat until late October. We become the most isolated humans there are - even the astronauts in the ISS are more connected to civilization with 24/7 internet and the ability to return to earth in an emergency. For over 8 months, we are alone. No more fresh food coming in, no way home even if you fall ill. Once the single sunset of the year occurs in March, we enter our 6 month long polar night, where temperatures can hit an ambient -100F. 

Here are some photos to document the year. Shot on a pixel 7a unless otherwise noted.

10_result.avif: The pole sits on a massive ice shelf that slowly moves across the continent. As such, the station (and everything on the surfaces) moves away from the true pole. The geographic pole marker and sign are adjusted back to true 90°S once a year. The ceremonial pole - this picture - is kept in front of the station. The flags are the original signatories of the Antarctic Treaty.
